Processing the Images

Satellites do not work like cameras. Instead of capturing a single scene that closely resembles what we would see with our eyes, a satellite sensor collects data about how different wavelengths of light are reflected off the Earth at every pixel in the image. Therefore, we are not simply taking pictures from space, but rather, start with raw data collected by NASA's Landsat 8 Satellite and meticulously process it into informative and visually striking representations of chlorophyll-a data.

Spectral Bands

The Landsat 8 collects data about how light is reflected from the Earth's surface at eleven different wavelengths. These are called spectral bands.

Interpreting the Data

We then use a program called Seadas to convert the raw data into information about phytoplankton concentration. Seadas applies complex algorithms discovered through years of experiments to the spectral band data and returns accurate estimates of chlorophyll-a concentration in the ocean. All phytoplankton contain the photosynthetic pigment chlorophyll-a, so this is the diagnostic pigment we use to generalize the abundance of them in the water.

Corrections

The outputs from Seadas are riddled with striping and imperfections. We use MATLAB to create stripe corrected verions of the images. However, these often have striping in other places. We combine the Seadas exported images and the MATLAB stripe corrected ones in photoshop to create a final image with the least amount of striping.
