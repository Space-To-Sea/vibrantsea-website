About the Project

Space to Sea: Vibrant Ocean Plankton Visualizations

The Space to Sea Vibrant Sea Ocean Plankton Visualization project is led by Keith Ellenbogen in collaboration with a group of students from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology and Wellesley College. At MIT Sea Grant, Ellenbogen and the team process raw satellite data from NASA to reveal the microscopic ocean plankton surrounding New England. Through a series of scientific visualizations that range from natural true-color imagery to vibrant artistic renderings, the project transforms satellite observations into works that communicate the beauty, diversity, and ecological significance of ocean plankton. We are also developing long-term visualizations that compare satellite observations across seasons and years to reveal changes in the presence of different plankton groups over time.

Our ocean planet is a living canvas in which microscopic plankton are so abundant they can transform the color of the sea surface. At first glance, the ocean appears a solid blue, the result of the red spectrum of visible light being absorbed by water. However, the ocean is not a solid color, but rather an ever-changing gradient of blue and green hues. New England's ocean is often described as emerald green, the result of an abundance of ocean-drifting plankton containing the pigment chlorophyll-a. From a human perspective on Earth, we are too close to the sea surface to observe the subtle color variations. But from the perspective of NASA Landsat 8 satellites orbiting through space, the ocean's surface can be viewed as a dynamic, ever-changing living palette of swirling brush strokes.

The visualizations range from true-color images that provide a realistic representation of the ocean and its plankton to chlorophyll images that apply a full-color spectrum to reveal the absence and intensity of chlorophyll-a concentrations. Other plankton visualizations combine a modern-day Andy Warhol-inspired color palette to communicate the abundance of microscopic organisms. Through haptophyte blues, dinoflagellate yellows, diatom purples, and green-algae greens, the processed satellite images become an artistic expression that reveals a deeper understanding of our environment at both the microscopic and planetary scales. Each visualization is a limited-edition work of art and an environmentally significant snapshot in time.
